# Fluid Mechanics Fundamentals: Questions & Answers

## 1. BASIC DEFINITIONS & CONCEPTS

### Q1: What is fluid mechanics?
**A:** Fluid mechanics is the study of fluid behavior (liquids, gases, blood, and plasmas) at rest and in motion. It encompasses:
- **Fluid Statics:** Study of fluids at rest
- **Fluid Dynamics:** Study of fluids in motion
- Applications: Mechanical engineering, chemical engineering, biological systems, astrophysics

### Q2: What are the three basic principles of fluid mechanics?
**A:** 
1. **Continuity Equation** (Mass Conservation)
2. **Momentum Principle** (Momentum Conservation)
3. **Energy Equation** (Energy Conservation)

### Q3: Define a fluid. What distinguishes it from a solid?
**A:** A fluid is a substance that cannot resist a shearing stress when at rest. Distinguishing features:
- **Solids:** Resist both normal and shearing stresses; have definite shape and volume
- **Liquids:** Resist normal stress; take shape of container; volume relatively constant
- **Gases:** Resist normal stress; expand to fill container; compressible
- **Defining property:** Fluids deform continuously under shearing stress

### Q4: What is an ideal fluid?
**A:** An ideal fluid is one that:
- Obeys Newton's law of viscosity
- Is frictionless (zero viscosity)
- Is incompressible (constant density)
- Note: No real fluid is truly ideal, but approximation useful for analysis

## 2. FLUID PROPERTIES

### Q5: Define density and specific gravity. What are the typical units?
**A:**
- **Density (ρ):** Mass per unit volume
  - Units: kg/m³ (SI) or slugs/ft³ (US customary)
  - Water at 4°C: 1000 kg/m³ (reference standard)
  
- **Specific Gravity (s):** Ratio of density of substance to density of water
  - Dimensionless quantity
  - Formula: s = ρ_substance / ρ_water
  - Example: Mercury has s ≈ 13.6 (13.6 times denser than water)

### Q6: What is kinematic viscosity? How does it differ from dynamic viscosity?
**A:**
- **Dynamic Viscosity (μ):** Measure of a fluid's resistance to flow under shear stress
  - Units: Pa·s (Pascal-seconds) or poise (1 Pa·s = 10 poise)
  - Symbol: μ or η
  
- **Kinematic Viscosity (ν):** Ratio of dynamic viscosity to density
  - Formula: ν = μ / ρ
  - Units: m²/s or stoke (1 m²/s = 10⁴ stoke)
  - Physical significance: Describes how quickly viscosity diffuses

### Q7: How does viscosity of gases and liquids vary with temperature?
**A:**
- **Liquids:** Viscosity DECREASES with increase in temperature
  - Reason: Increased molecular activity reduces intermolecular cohesion
  - Example: Honey flows faster when heated
  
- **Gases:** Viscosity INCREASES with increase in temperature
  - Reason: Increased molecular motion increases momentum exchange
  - Explanation: Gas molecules interact more vigorously at higher temperature

### Q8: What is surface tension? Give examples of its effects.
**A:**
- **Definition:** Force per unit length acting on the surface of a liquid, causing it to behave like an elastic membrane
- **Units:** N/m (Newtons per meter) or dyne/cm
- **Cause:** Unbalanced intermolecular forces at liquid-gas interface
- **Examples:**
  - Water droplets form spheres (minimum surface area)
  - Capillary rise in narrow tubes
  - Insects can walk on water
  - Formation of bubbles and foam

### Q9: What is vapor pressure? Why is it important in fluid mechanics?
**A:**
- **Definition:** Pressure exerted by a vapor in equilibrium with its liquid at a given temperature
- **Importance in fluid mechanics:**
  - Cavitation: When local pressure drops below vapor pressure, bubbles form and collapse
  - Causes damage to pumps and hydraulic structures
  - Limits maximum suction height of pumps
  - Critical in design of marine propellers and turbines

### Q10: Define compressibility and bulk modulus.
**A:**
- **Compressibility:** Measure of how much a substance's volume changes under pressure
  - Higher compressibility = easier to compress
  
- **Bulk Modulus (K):** Inverse of compressibility
  - Definition: Ratio of pressure change to fractional volume change
  - Formula: K = -ΔP / (ΔV/V)
  - Units: Pa (Pascals)
  - For water: K ≈ 2.2 × 10⁹ Pa (relatively incompressible)
  - For air: K ≈ 1.01 × 10⁵ Pa (very compressible)

## 3. FLUID CLASSIFICATION

### Q11: Classify fluids based on viscosity behavior.
**A:**
- **Newtonian Fluids:** Follow Newton's law of viscosity (constant viscosity)
  - Examples: Water, oil, air, most common fluids
  - Relationship: τ = μ(du/dy)
  
- **Non-Newtonian Fluids:** Viscosity changes with shear rate
  - **Pseudoplastic (Shear-thinning):** Viscosity decreases with shear rate
    - Examples: Paint, ketchup, blood, polymer solutions
    - Advantage: Flow easier at high shear rates
    
  - **Dilatant (Shear-thickening):** Viscosity increases with shear rate
    - Examples: Sand-water mixture, oobleck, quicksand
    - Behavior: Becomes more rigid under stress
    
  - **Bingham Plastic:** Requires threshold stress before flowing
    - Examples: Toothpaste, mud, sewage sludge
    - Behavior: Solid until stress exceeds yield stress

### Q12: What is a scalar quantity in fluid mechanics?
**A:** A scalar quantity has only magnitude, no direction.

**Examples of scalar quantities:**
- **Pressure (P):** Force per unit area (same in all directions at a point)
- **Density (ρ):** Mass per unit volume
- **Temperature (T):** Measure of thermal energy
- **Velocity magnitude (speed):** Magnitude of velocity vector
- **Viscosity (μ):** Measure of resistance to flow

**Note:** Vector quantities have both magnitude and direction:
- Velocity, acceleration, force, displacement

## 4. PRESSURE & HYDROSTATICS

### Q13: State Pascal's Law. What are its applications?
**A:**
- **Pascal's Law:** Pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished in all directions and to all surfaces of the containing vessel

**Applications:**
1. **Hydraulic systems** (car brakes, lifts, presses)
2. **Pressure transducers**
3. **Fluid power transmission**
4. **Hydraulic press:** Small input force creates large output force (F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂)
5. **Dams and pressure vessels:** Pressure acts equally in all directions

### Q14: What is hydrostatic pressure? How does it vary with depth?
**A:**
- **Definition:** Pressure at a point within a stationary fluid due to the weight of fluid above it
- **Formula:** P = ρgh
  - ρ = density of fluid
  - g = acceleration due to gravity
  - h = depth below surface
  
- **Variation with depth:**
  - Increases linearly with depth
  - Independent of container shape (paradox: same pressure at same depth in different containers)
  - For water: Pressure increases by 1 atm (≈101.3 kPa) for every 10 m depth

### Q15: What is pressure head?
**A:**
- **Definition:** Height of a column of fluid that would produce the same pressure
- **Formula:** h = P / (ρg)
- **Units:** meters of fluid (m H₂O, m Hg, etc.)
- **Example:** 
  - Atmospheric pressure at sea level: 101.3 kPa = 10.33 m H₂O = 760 mm Hg
  - Useful for expressing pressure in terms of column height

## 5. ARCHIMEDES' PRINCIPLE & BUOYANCY

### Q16: State Archimedes' Principle.
**A:** The buoyant force on an object immersed in a fluid is equal to the weight of the fluid displaced by the object, acting upward through the center of buoyancy.

**Mathematical expression:**
- Buoyant Force (F_B) = ρ_fluid × V_displaced × g
- F_B = Weight of fluid displaced

**Applications:**
- Ship floating: Weight = Buoyant force
- Submarines: Adjust ballast to control buoyancy
- Helium balloons: Rise because buoyant force > weight
- Hydrometer: Measures specific gravity by float depth

### Q17: Under what conditions does an object float, sink, or remain suspended?
**A:**
- **Float:** Weight < Buoyant force (Object density < Fluid density)
- **Sink:** Weight > Buoyant force (Object density > Fluid density)
- **Suspend (Neutral buoyancy):** Weight = Buoyant force (Object density = Fluid density)

**Condition for floating:**
- ρ_object × V_total < ρ_fluid × V_submerged

## 6. MANOMETERS & PRESSURE MEASUREMENT

### Q18: What is a manometer? Describe different types.
**A:**
- **Definition:** Device using liquid column to measure pressure difference

**Types:**

1. **Simple Manometer (U-tube):**
   - Measures gauge pressure
   - One end open to atmosphere, other to system
   - Pressure difference: ΔP = ρgh (where h = height difference)

2. **Differential Manometer:**
   - Measures pressure difference between two points
   - Both ends connected to different pressure sources
   - ΔP = ρ_manometer × g × (h₁ - h₂)

3. **Inclined Manometer:**
   - More sensitive than vertical U-tube
   - Used for small pressure differences
   - Manometer tube at angle to horizontal

4. **Micromanometer:**
   - Extremely sensitive
   - Can measure very small pressure differences

## 7. REYNOLDS NUMBER & FLOW CLASSIFICATION

### Q19: What is Reynolds number? What is its significance?
**A:**
- **Definition:** Dimensionless number comparing inertial forces to viscous forces
- **Formula:** Re = ρVD/μ = VD/ν
  - V = velocity
  - D = characteristic length (pipe diameter)
  - μ = dynamic viscosity
  - ν = kinematic viscosity

- **Significance:** Determines type of flow (laminar vs turbulent)

### Q20: Classify flow based on Reynolds number.
**A:**
- **Laminar Flow:** Re < 2,300 (for pipe flow)
  - Smooth, orderly motion
  - Fluid moves in parallel layers
  - No mixing between layers
  - Velocity profile parabolic
  
- **Transitional Flow:** 2,300 < Re < 4,000
  - Flow intermittently changes between laminar and turbulent
  
- **Turbulent Flow:** Re > 4,000
  - Chaotic, irregular motion
  - Eddies and vortices present
  - Significant mixing between layers
  - Velocity profile more uniform
  - Higher pressure drop than laminar

## 8. TYPES OF FLUID FLOW

### Q21: Define and distinguish between steady and unsteady flow.
**A:**
- **Steady Flow:** Flow properties at any given point do NOT change with time
  - ∂(properties)/∂t = 0
  - Example: Constant water flow from a tap
  
- **Unsteady Flow:** Flow properties at a point change with time
  - ∂(properties)/∂t ≠ 0
  - Example: Starting a pump, closing a valve, tidal flows

### Q22: Define uniform and non-uniform flow.
**A:**
- **Uniform Flow:** Velocity is the same at all points in the flow field at a given instant
  - Example: Flow in long straight pipe with constant diameter
  
- **Non-uniform Flow:** Velocity varies from point to point in the flow field
  - Example: Flow around a sphere, flow in expanding pipe

### Q23: Define rotational and irrotational flow.
**A:**
- **Irrotational Flow:** Fluid particles do NOT rotate about their own axes
  - Vorticity = 0
  - Curl of velocity vector = 0
  - Example: Flow around a smooth sphere, potential flow
  
- **Rotational Flow:** Fluid particles rotate about their own axes
  - Vorticity ≠ 0
  - Example: Flow with boundary layers, wake flows

### Q24: What is one-dimensional, two-dimensional, and three-dimensional flow?
**A:**
- **One-dimensional (1D) Flow:** Properties depend on one spatial coordinate only
  - Example: Fully developed pipe flow (depends only on radius)
  
- **Two-dimensional (2D) Flow:** Properties depend on two spatial coordinates
  - Example: Flow between parallel plates, flow around cylinder
  
- **Three-dimensional (3D) Flow:** Properties depend on all three spatial coordinates
  - Example: Real flows around most objects
